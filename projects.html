Air Quality Forecasting Model

Organization: Northeast Ohio Areawide Coordinating Agency (NOACA)

Timeline: 2013-2014

Role: Lead Analyst & Model Developer

Tools: SPSS, Excel, SQL, Statistical Modeling

Challenge

Regional planning agency needed an accurate system to predict air quality advisory days for particulate matter (PM2.5) and ozone to protect vulnerable populations and comply with EPA requirements. Existing STI model had limited predictive accuracy.

Approach

  • Collected and merged multiple data sources: meteorological data, historical air quality measurements, emission inventories, and seasonal patterns
  • Cleaned data addressing missing values, temporal inconsistencies, and measurement errors across different monitoring stations
  • Conducted exploratory data analysis to identify key predictor variables and correlations
  • Developed statistical forecasting model using regression analysis and time-series methods
  • Validated model performance against historical data and compared to existing STI model
  • Created documentation for model methodology, data sources, and quality assurance procedures

Results & Impact

  • Replaced existing STI model with improved prediction accuracy for next-day air quality levels
  • Enabled proactive public health messaging to vulnerable populations (children, elderly, those with respiratory conditions)
  • Supported regional compliance with National Ambient Air Quality Standards (NAAQS)
  • Model used daily for operational forecasting and informed State Implementation Plan (SIP) development
  • Presented findings at quarterly stakeholder meetings to policymakers, scientists, and community leaders

Key Skills Demonstrated

  • Complex data cleaning and merging from disparate sources
  • Statistical modeling for predictive analytics
  • Time-series analysis and trend identification
  • Reproducible research methodology and documentation
  • Translation of technical findings for diverse stakeholders
  • Real-world application with direct public health impact

Cloud Cover Variability in the Great Lakes Region

Organization: Kent State University (Master's Thesis)

Timeline: 2008-2009

Role: Principal Researcher

Tools: SPSS, ArcGIS, Excel, Remote Sensing, Statistical Analysis

Research Question

How does cloud cover vary spatially and temporally across the Great Lakes region, and what atmospheric and geographic factors drive these patterns? Understanding these relationships has implications for climate modeling, agricultural planning, and regional weather prediction.

Methodology

  • Compiled multi-year dataset of satellite-derived cloud cover measurements across Great Lakes basin
  • Cleaned and standardized data from multiple sensors and temporal periods
  • Conducted geospatial analysis using ArcGIS to map regional patterns and create 88 finalized maps
  • Applied statistical methods including correlation analysis, regression modeling, and spatial autocorrelation tests
  • Analyzed relationships between cloud cover and variables: lake effect patterns, topography, seasonal cycles, and synoptic weather patterns
  • Generated reproducible workflows for data processing and analysis

Key Findings

  • Identified significant spatial patterns in cloud cover related to lake proximity and topographic features
  • Quantified seasonal and diurnal variations in cloud formation across the region
  • Demonstrated statistical relationships between atmospheric circulation patterns and regional cloud cover
  • Produced 163-page thesis documenting methodology, analysis, and findings

Dissemination

  • Presented research at International Association for Great Lakes Research (IAGLR) conference
  • Presented at multiple Association of American Geographers (AAG) annual meetings
  • Contributed to understanding of regional climate patterns and weather forecasting

Relevance to Healthcare Analytics

  • Demonstrates ability to design and execute complex quantitative research independently
  • Experience with large-scale data cleaning, quality control, and validation
  • Proficiency in spatial analysis methods applicable to healthcare geography and disparities research
  • Strong documentation and reproducibility practices essential for research environments
  • Experience presenting complex analytical findings to academic and professional audiences
